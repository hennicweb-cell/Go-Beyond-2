How the search was conducted

A talent pool of 317 candidates was identified through proactive headhunting, sector network mapping, and targeted search across the charity fundraising community. This included direct outreach to individual giving specialists, supporter engagement and communications managers, regular giving programme managers, and multi-discipline fundraisers working across the sector nationally.

Over 35 discovery conversations were held with candidates identified through the search to assess initial suitability, motivation, and fit against the brief. From those, 20 candidates were invited to first interview and 10 progressed to competency interview.

Three candidates are recommended for your consideration and are presented in this report. A fourth candidate — an internal Go Beyond applicant — has also been advanced to the client interview stage as part of a fair and consistent process, with a full assessment included below.

Note on the shortlist

Four candidates were originally identified for the client interview stage. One candidate was subsequently unable to attend on the agreed dates. Three external candidates are formally recommended. The internal candidate (Jasmine Manders) is included in the process as a matter of fair practice. A separate assessment note accompanies her profile below.

Assessment Framework & Important Notes

Please read before reviewing scores

The assessments, scores and commentary in this report represent one recruiter's interpretation of each candidate's performance across CV review, first interview and structured competency interview. They are not a definitive assessment of any candidate's capability or potential.

Scores are based on specific evidence gathered through the recruitment process — including written application, responses to set interview questions, and structured competency interview responses. Candidates may perform differently in a face-to-face setting, under different conditions, or when responding to questions framed in a way that suits their natural communication style.

This report is intended as a guide to inform your own assessment at interview — not as a final verdict. The scores and commentary should prompt lines of enquiry, not close them down. The final assessment and hiring decision rests entirely with you.

How scores are calculated

Each candidate is assessed across six dimensions, each carrying a different weighting based on the priorities of this brief. A score out of 10 is given for each dimension, multiplied by the weighting to produce a weighted score. Total scores are out of 100.

Scores draw on four sources of evidence: CV and written application, cover letter (where submitted), first interview, and structured competency interview. Interview evidence carries the heaviest weight. STAR format delivery is not scored — substance and capability are. Nerves are taken into account throughout.

Competency Interview Questions & Rationale
The same eight questions were asked of all candidates in the same order. Questions were designed to test the specific capabilities identified as critical to success in this role.
B1 — Behavioural"Tell me about a time you owned a fundraising or marketing campaign end-to-end — from strategy through to results — where something went wrong mid-campaign. What did you do, and what was the outcome?"
Why we asked it: Go Beyond's campaigns — Ice Cream Moments, appeals, challenge events — run with small teams and limited resource. Things go wrong. This question tests whether candidates have genuinely owned a campaign from start to finish and can manage in real time when circumstances change. The crisis element is deliberate — it reveals decision-making under pressure and the difference between people who manage campaigns and people who execute within them.
B2 — Behavioural"Describe a time you built or grew an income stream — or a supporter base — from very little. What did you prioritise first, and what did you actually deliver?"
Why we asked it: Go Beyond's regular giving programme, legacy pipeline, and alumni engagement are all at early stages. The person in this role will be building, not maintaining. This question tests builder instinct — whether candidates have genuinely created something from nothing or have managed what already existed.
B3 — Behavioural"Give me an example of a time you had to get up to speed in a fundraising or marketing discipline that wasn't your strongest. What did you do to develop, and what did you deliver as a result?"
Why we asked it: The role spans individual giving, regular giving, legacy, challenge events, database management, and line management. No candidate will arrive with equal depth across all. This question tests learning agility — whether candidates can pick up a new discipline under real pressure and deliver a concrete outcome.
B4 — Behavioural"Tell me about the most challenging people management situation you've faced. What was the situation, how did you handle it, and what would you do differently with hindsight?"
Why we asked it: This role has two direct reports who need clear direction and development. Previous post-holders have not managed this effectively. This question tests whether candidates have genuinely managed difficult people situations — not just mentored willing colleagues — and whether they have a professional and considered approach to doing so.
F1 — Functional"Go Beyond uses Raiser's Edge. If Sara asked you on day one to pull a segment of regular givers who have donated consistently for three or more years but haven't given in the last six months — walk me through exactly how you'd approach that, and what you'd do with the data once you had it."
Why we asked it: Long-standing regular givers who have recently lapsed represent one of the highest-value reactivation opportunities in the database. This question tests both data literacy (can candidates describe the correct query logic in Raiser's Edge?) and strategic thinking (do they know what to do with the segment once they have it?). Candidates who can describe the methodology AND the reactivation approach demonstrate genuine CRM capability.
F2 — Functional"Go Beyond has around 200–300 regular givers generating £90k a year. There is no acquisition programme and no ring-fenced budget — every spend needs a business case to Sara. What would your first regular giving acquisition proposal look like, what would you spend, and how would you justify the ROI?"
Why we asked it: Every spend in this role needs a business case. This question tests financial instinct — whether candidates can construct a structured acquisition proposal with channel rationale, cost logic, audience segmentation, projected conversion, and ROI framing. Candidates who answer in activity terms rather than income terms flag a significant concern.
F3 — Functional"Ice Cream Moments needs to generate £20,000 this year at £3.50 per virtual ice cream, with celebrity endorsement from Louis Theroux, James Acaster and Jenny Agutter. Walk me through how you'd plan to hit that target — channels, audience, and what you'd do mid-campaign if it looked like you were falling short."
Why we asked it: ICM is Go Beyond's flagship summer campaign and one of the first things the new person will need to either continue or build on. This question tests creative campaign thinking, channel planning, celebrity asset activation, and mid-campaign decision-making. It also tests whether candidates have actually researched Go Beyond before interview — ICM is prominent on the website and in the charity's communications.
F4 — Functional"Go Beyond's regular givers currently receive nothing beyond appeal letters. You have one month and no external resource — no agency, no designer, no freelancer. Design me a basic stewardship journey for those donors. Walk me through every step, every touchpoint, and who sends what, when."
Why we asked it: The regular giving stewardship gap is the highest-priority income opportunity in the role. This question tests whether candidates understand that stewardship is a journey, not a transaction — and whether they can design one specifically for Go Beyond's supporter base. The no-external-resource constraint is deliberate: it tests in-house execution capability and creative thinking within real constraints.

Interview Guidance
Suggested lines of enquiry for the client interview. These are prompts, not scripts — use them to open conversations rather than close them.
Income accountability — the most important line of enquiry
Sara has described two previous hires who performed well at interview but could not translate ideas into income delivery. The question that most reliably separates candidates who own income from those who contribute to someone else's income is: "What was your income target, who set it, how was it broken down, what did you actually hit — and what did you do in the month when it looked like you would fall short?" All three recommended candidates can answer this question with specific numbers. The quality and specificity of the answer matters as much as the numbers themselves.
Ideas into plans — Sara's primary concern
Previous hires presented brilliantly and then could not operationalise their ideas. The antidote is to probe every strategic answer with: "Walk me through exactly how you would execute that — who does what, by when, what does success look like at 30 days and 90 days, and what would tell you it is not working?" Candidates who can go from idea to plan in a single breath demonstrate the operational instinct the role needs. Candidates who stay at the level of strategy are a concern.
Managing the team — the practical management question
The Digital Marketing Officer needs active management and development from week one. A useful question for each candidate: "Imagine it is week three and one of your direct reports is not yet performing to the standard you need. Walk me through what you do — step by step, starting with day one of that conversation." The answer reveals whether candidates understand formal performance management or are defaulting to informal coaching. All three recommended candidates have some management evidence — the depth varies and is noted in each profile above.
Go Beyond knowledge — a signal of genuine engagement
All three recommended candidates have researched Go Beyond in depth. A useful opening question: "Tell me what you know about Go Beyond — and tell me what you noticed, as a fundraiser, when you looked at the income picture." The second half of this question — the fundraiser's observation — is where the best candidates will distinguish themselves. You are looking for someone who has looked at the regular giving base, the legacy programme, the ICM campaign, and the alumni potential through the lens of income opportunity, not just organisational description.
The live task — what to watch for
The task tests three things simultaneously: people management thinking, database and systems thinking, and creative campaign thinking under pressure. Watch for: how candidates prioritise when they cannot cover everything; whether they use the data available to them or ignore it; whether they talk to everyone in the room or only to Sara and Michelle; and whether their campaign ideas arrive with income logic attached or stay at the level of activity and reach. The questions that arise from the task presentation should feed directly into the formal interview.
