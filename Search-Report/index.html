How the search was conducted

A talent pool of 317 candidates was identified through proactive headhunting, sector network mapping, and targeted search across the charity fundraising community. This included direct outreach to individual giving specialists, supporter engagement and communications managers, regular giving programme managers, and multi-discipline fundraisers working nationally.

Over 35 discovery conversations were held with candidates to assess initial suitability, motivation, and fit against the brief. From those, 20 candidates were invited to first interview and 10 progressed to competency interview. Four candidates are recommended for your consideration and are presented in this report, including one internal Go Beyond applicant who has been advanced through the same process as a matter of fair practice.

Assessment Framework & Important Notes

Please read before reviewing scores

The assessments, scores and commentary in this report represent one recruiter's interpretation of each candidate's performance across CV review, first interview and structured competency interview. They are not a definitive assessment of any candidate's capability or potential.

Scores are based on specific evidence gathered through the recruitment process — including written application, responses to set interview questions, and structured competency interview responses. Candidates may perform differently in a face-to-face setting, under different conditions, or when responding to questions framed in a way that suits their natural communication style.

This report is intended as a guide to inform your own assessment at interview — not as a final verdict. The scores and commentary should prompt lines of enquiry, not close them down. The final assessment and hiring decision rests entirely with you.

How scores are calculated

Each candidate is assessed across six dimensions, each carrying a different weighting based on the priorities of this brief. A score out of 10 is given for each dimension, multiplied by the weighting to produce a weighted score. Total scores are out of 100. Scores draw on four sources of evidence: CV, cover letter (where submitted), first interview, and competency interview. Interview evidence carries the heaviest weight. STAR format delivery is not scored — substance and capability are. Nerves are taken into account throughout.

Competency Interview Questions & Rationale
The same eight questions were asked of all candidates in the same order.
B1 — Behavioural"Tell me about a time you owned a fundraising or marketing campaign end-to-end — from strategy through to results — where something went wrong mid-campaign. What did you do, and what was the outcome?"
Why we asked it: Tests whether candidates have genuinely owned a campaign from start to finish and can make decisions in real time when circumstances change. The crisis element reveals decision-making under pressure.
B2 — Behavioural"Describe a time you built or grew an income stream — or a supporter base — from very little. What did you prioritise first, and what did you actually deliver?"
Why we asked it: Tests builder instinct — whether candidates have genuinely created something from nothing, or managed what already existed. Go Beyond's regular giving programme, legacy pipeline and alumni engagement all need building.
B3 — Behavioural"Give me an example of a time you had to get up to speed in a fundraising or marketing discipline that wasn't your strongest. What did you do to develop, and what did you deliver as a result?"
Why we asked it: Tests learning agility. The role spans individual giving, regular giving, legacy, challenge events, database management, and line management. No candidate arrives with equal depth across all.
B4 — Behavioural"Tell me about the most challenging people management situation you've faced. What was the situation, how did you handle it, and what would you do differently with hindsight?"
Why we asked it: Tests whether candidates have genuinely managed difficult people situations and have a professional approach to doing so. The role has two direct reports requiring clear direction from day one.
F1 — Functional"Go Beyond uses Raiser's Edge. If Sara asked you on day one to pull a segment of regular givers who have donated consistently for three or more years but haven't given in the last six months — walk me through exactly how you'd approach that, and what you'd do with the data once you had it."
Why we asked it: Tests data literacy (correct query logic) and strategic thinking (what to do with the segment). Long-standing regular givers who have recently lapsed represent one of the highest-value reactivation opportunities in the database.
F2 — Functional"Go Beyond has around 200–300 regular givers generating £90k a year. There is no acquisition programme and no ring-fenced budget — every spend needs a business case to Sara. What would your first regular giving acquisition proposal look like, what would you spend, and how would you justify the ROI?"
Why we asked it: Tests financial instinct. Candidates who answer in activity terms rather than income terms — no cost, no conversion rate, no ROI framing — flag the same concern as previous failed hires in this role.
F3 — Functional"Ice Cream Moments needs to generate £20,000 this year at £3.50 per virtual ice cream, with celebrity endorsement from Louis Theroux, James Acaster and Jenny Agutter. Walk me through how you'd plan to hit that target — channels, audience, and what you'd do mid-campaign if it looked like you were falling short."
Why we asked it: Tests creative campaign thinking, celebrity asset activation, channel planning and mid-campaign decision-making. Also tests whether candidates have actually researched Go Beyond before interview.
F4 — Functional"Go Beyond's regular givers currently receive nothing beyond appeal letters. You have one month and no external resource — no agency, no designer, no freelancer. Design me a basic stewardship journey for those donors. Walk me through every step, every touchpoint, and who sends what, when."
Why we asked it: Tests whether candidates understand stewardship as a relationship journey rather than a transaction. The no-external-resource constraint tests in-house execution capability. The regular giving stewardship gap is the highest-priority income opportunity in the role.
