What We Know — The Situation
Context

Go Beyond is a 32-year-old children's charity that gives vulnerable kids — young carers, children in poverty, those bereaved or bullied — life-changing residential breaks at centres in Cornwall and Derbyshire. Entirely self-funded at £1.8m per year. No government money. Everything fundraised.

Why this hire is happening

Two failed hires in this role in quick succession. Both interviewed brilliantly. Both fell apart in post. Sara is now Director and personally holding the function together alongside her Trusts Manager. The role has been vacant long enough that the team is stretched and certain activities — notably the newsletter, proper challenge event stewardship, and any structured regular giver programme — have effectively stalled.

Hire 1 — "Izzy"
Fired — ~6 months

Background: charity marketing, then moved into fundraising. CV claimed she'd run direct marketing campaigns that brought in new regular givers. Sara suspects she was adjacent to those campaigns, not running them.

The data person at the time was strong and largely sorted the database — but Izzy had no understanding of what was happening and actually set it back when she got involved.

Hire 2 — "Lena"
Let go — ~5 months

Background: community fundraising, claimed experience in direct marketing and challenge events. Did a live roundtable task in the final interview — was brilliant. Said nothing in team meetings once she started.

Gave a Mailchimp plugin to a developer which mass-imported the entire database onto the mailing list (thousands without contact info). Didn't do a newsletter. Database was left in a mess.

Key insight from Sara

"I think we knew quite early on with Izzy and with Lena where the red flags were. But it's very hard in HR to prove it… you have to give them a chance." Both were neurodivergent — Sara flagged this not as a reason, but as context. Both also claimed direct marketing campaign experience they likely didn't own. The CVs sounded right. The interviews were convincing. The execution wasn't there.

2
Year One — What Sara Actually Needs

Sara's exact words: "I want them to be coming to me with ideas and then putting them in place." The three priorities she named:

1

Own the Database — Strategy, Not Just Operations

Not just keeping data ticking over — coming in with a strategy. What's wrong with it? What do we need it to do? Setting up a monthly data dashboard with income analysis by segment. Being able to say "the appeal underperformed because the £10-£20 donor ask wasn't prominent enough in the letter." The previous person actually set the database back when she tried to engage with it. Sara wants someone who leads from the front here.

2

Build Supporter Journeys — All Income Streams

No proper supporter journeys currently exist for regular givers, challenge event runners, or Ice Cream Moments donors. Sara gave a specific example: the ICM campaign offered a virtual ice cream tribute — no one had tested the donor journey or thought about how the recipient receives it, or how they share it. The right person designs the journey, tests it, then improves it. "Half a day to write it — it's not rocket science."

3

Write a Strategy + Build Pipelines

An 18-month to 2-year delivery plan covering challenge events, legacies, appeals, regular givers and the database. Proper pipelines. "The previous people just wrote an appeal letter, didn't matter — whereas there are targets attached." Sara wants forecast thinking: "We need 20 more regular givers, we'll get them from Ice Cream Moments, that brings in £X — and once they've given, we'll uplift 10% of them."

4

Launch the Legacy Programme

Infrastructure already exists and is ready to go. No one has had capacity to push it live. "We've got an ageing supporter base and we don't do any active promotion for legacy." Sara specifically called this out as a first-six-months win. Free Wills Week, targeted comms to older regular givers — all of it sits with this person.

▲ Promising — Just Starting

Starting in 2 weeks, based in Chippenham. Came from another charity where she managed their Raiser's Edge database. During screening, proactively sent examples of challenge event supporter journey letters she'd used in her previous role. This is the person who will be the database operator day-to-day. If she's strong on RE, the structural vulnerability that brought the last hire down is reduced — but the Supporter Care Manager still needs to be able to oversee and run queries themselves.

Wider team as resource

Sara was clear: when campaigns like Ice Cream Moments are running, the whole fundraising team pitches in — trust fundraisers write social content, everyone gets involved. This is cultural. The new person won't just have two direct reports — they'll have the energy of a seven-person team behind the big moments. "It gives them something else to do and wider experience."

Sara's Management Style

  • Structured two-week induction with daily morning check-ins
  • Daily Teams contact until the person doesn't need it — Sara's phrase: "until they don't want me to"
  • Formal monthly one-to-ones throughout probation
  • Very relaxed style day-to-day — chatty, collaborative, "brains trust" culture ("what do you think of this idea?")
  • Trusts Manager and Corporate Manager both speak to Sara multiple times a day on Teams
  • Six-month probation period with formal review
  • Annual pay review benchmarked against sector (January delivery)

Candidate Profile & Non-Negotiables
Sara's view on background

"It's a vast job with such a big umbrella covering a lot — I'm not expecting anyone to be great at all things. What they need is to go: 'I can't do that, but I could manage that.'" She explicitly said CVs will always have something slightly missing. Attitude and logical/strategic thinking matter as much as experience. Her corporate fundraiser came from a stressful corporate environment and brought a fresh approach — Sara is open to that model here too.

Open to

Commercial direct marketing backgrounds. Agency-side experience (DMS, Wood for Trees, Salison Group etc). Individual giving managers with marcomms experience. People who have not managed a team before but are willing to invest in management training (British Growth Fund currently running a 6-week programme pro bono for Go Beyond). Charity sector not essential.

Non-Negotiables

Financially literate about targets
Sara's example: ICM is £20k at £3.50 per virtual ice cream. They need to know how many they need, then think about how to uplift. "The last two didn't even ask what the targets were. Didn't do the maths." Must be aware they are a fundraiser, not just a marketer.
Hands-on as well as strategic
Sara's words: "If they couldn't pull a report up on the database themselves, how are they going to train someone up on it?" Must be operational, not purely conceptual. Can direct and delegate — but can also do.
Execution, not just ideas
The core failure of both previous hires. Both gave brilliant presentations. Neither delivered. Must be able to show evidence of campaigns or projects they owned end-to-end — with dates, outputs, results.
Data literacy
Not expert-level — but genuinely comfortable in Raiser's Edge (or equivalent CRM), able to run queries, understand segmentation, and understand data quality. Can oversee without being dependent on the data person.
⚠ Dealbreaker: purely strategic only
If they cannot be hands-on. If they see database work as beneath them. If they can only think at the macro level and rely on others to implement everything. That's the pattern that's already failed twice.
Management inexperience — not a dealbreaker
Sara's view: "We can get them a lot of training, a lot of support." The British Growth Fund management programme is available. More important that they have the right instincts and attitude toward people than a formal track record.
The final stage task — redesigned

Previous presentations (prepared in advance, polished, brilliant) didn't predict performance. Sara now wants a live, unprepared task given on the day. She's done prepared task + roundtable — both passed, both failed in post. The new task should test thinking on their feet and specifically execution-mode thinking. Suggestion discussed: give them the regular givers scenario (£90k, no stewardship, 20% growth target) and ask for a plan — not a strategy doc — with month-by-month milestones and owners. 20 minutes, unprepared.

Louis Theroux, James Acaster (he's obsessed with ice cream), David Gower, Jenny Agutter all backing Ice Cream Moments. A small charity with national-calibre celebrity endorsement.

The supporter care function essentially doesn't exist yet. No one has done this properly. For the right person, that's an extraordinary opportunity — not a problem. "It wouldn't take much."

The whole business shuts down Friday afternoons. 28 days holiday plus bank holidays. Annual pay review benchmarked against sector. Sara described this as a genuine USP: "that's a really good seller."

Sara: "The ones that like it, like it because of the team and the team atmosphere." Previous hires who've succeeded describe a genuinely supportive environment with no toxic management or unrealistic targets.

Fully remote. But can work from a coastal cottage at the Cornwall centre for a week. Centres in Cornwall and Derbyshire — not just Zoom.
